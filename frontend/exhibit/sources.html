Poster 9 · National Youth Champions

LIGHTHOUSE WINS THE McGUIRE CUP

  1. Encyclopedia of Greater Philadelphia — Ed Farnsworth, “Soccer” (Rutgers University, 2018) — peer-reviewed scholarly entry confirming Lighthouse Boys Club as McGuire Cup champions in 1938, 1948, 1949, 1957, and 1967, and characterizing the run as “five times over a thirty-year period”: philadelphiaencyclopedia.org/essays/soccer
  2. The Philadelphia Soccer Page — Ed Farnsworth, “Great Philly Soccer Teams: Lighthouse” (McGuire Cup years 1938, 1948, 1949, 1957, 1967): phillysoccerpage.net/2010/01/07/great-philly-soccer-teams-lighthouse
  3. Society for American Soccer History — Len Oliver, “Philadelphia Soccer in the 1940s and 1950s, Part 2: Youth & Junior League Soccer with the Lighthouse Boys Club” (May 18, 2015) — Oliver’s first-person account of the back-to-back-champion Invincibles, the 36-game streak, and the Windsor S.C. comeback final, republished from his original 1992 Journal of Ethno-Development article: ussoccerhistory.org · Lighthouse / Len Oliver
  4. Society for American Soccer History — “Q&A with Len Oliver: Youth and Junior Soccer with Lighthouse Boys Club” (May 19, 2015) — source for the Lighthouse Juniors’ rival clubs (Nicetown, Thomas Celtic, Erzgebirge, plus Italian, Hungarian, Ukrainian, Polish, Latvian, and Armenian ethnic-club youth teams) and Tom Oliver as the Invincibles’ coach: ussoccerhistory.org · Len Oliver Q&A
  5. Wikipedia — Lighthouse Boys Club (national youth championships): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lighthouse_Boys_Club

Poster 10 · Scholastic & College

LIGHTHOUSE IN DIVISION 1 COLLEGE SOCCER

  1. Society for American Soccer History — Len Oliver, “Philadelphia Soccer in the 1940s and 1950s, Part 2: Youth & Junior League Soccer with the Lighthouse Boys Club” (May 18, 2015) — Oliver’s first-person account of the five Northeast High boys recruited on full scholarship by Pete Leaness at Temple, the 1951 national title vs. University of San Francisco at Kezar Stadium, the 1953 repeat, and the NSCAA All-American honors: ussoccerhistory.org · Lighthouse / Len Oliver

  3. Wikipedia — Walter Bahr — coaching career at Frankford High School, Temple University, and Penn State (14 seasons leading the Nittany Lions): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Bahr
  4. Wikipedia — Len Oliver (soccer) — Lighthouse Boys Club youth; Temple University NSCAA All-American (two-time); two-time pre-NCAA national intercollegiate champion (1951, 1953); National Soccer Hall of Fame 1996: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Len_Oliver_(soccer)

Poster 12 · Lighthouse Players in the Pros

LIGHTHOUSE IN THE PROS

  1. Wikipedia — Philadelphia Nationals (Fairhill F.C. era, 1942-1954): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia_Nationals
  2. Wikipedia — Philadelphia Ukrainians / Ukrainian Nationals (6 ASL titles, 4 U.S. Open Cups, 1978 Horsham move): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philadelphia_Ukrainians
  3. Wikipedia — Benny McLaughlin (Lighthouse to Philadelphia Nationals 1945-1954, four ASL titles): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benny_McLaughlin
  4. Wikipedia — Francis Ryan (Lighthouse Boys Club youth; post-WWII return to Philadelphia Nationals; “leading the team to the ASL Lewis Cup in 1948-49 and 1951-52”; National Soccer Hall of Fame 1958 inductee): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Ryan
  5. Wikipedia — Len Oliver (soccer) — Lighthouse Boys Club youth (1948 & 1949 U-19 National Champion); Uhrik Truckers 1955–57; U.S. national team at 1963 Pan American Games; National Soccer Hall of Fame 1996 inductee: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Len_Oliver_(soccer)
  6. Society for American Soccer History — Ed Farnsworth, “Len Oliver Q&A: Playing in the pros in 1950s Philadelphia and beyond” (June 25, 2015) — primary-source interview confirming the Philadelphia Nationals folded before Oliver could play pro for them, his signing with Jimmy Mills at the Uhrik Truckers for “$35 a game in a brown envelope,” and the archive’s description of the 1954-55 Uhrik Truckers championship side as “a group of relative unknowns, many of whom were products of the legendary Lighthouse Boys Club”: ussoccerhistory.org/len-oliver-qa-playing-in-the-pros-in-1950s-philadelphia-and-beyond
  7. Wikipedia — Ed McIlvenny (1949 ASL outstanding player, 1950 U.S. WC captain): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ed_McIlvenny

Poster 13 · At the Olympics

LIGHTHOUSE AT THE OLYMPICS

  1. Encyclopedia of Greater Philadelphia — Ed Farnsworth, “Soccer” (Rutgers University, 2018) — peer-reviewed scholarly entry: “Eight of the seventeen players on the first U.S. Olympic soccer team at the 1924 Games in Paris were also from Philadelphia teams,” and “alumni of the club played on the U.S. Men's National Team at the Olympics and World Cup”: philadelphiaencyclopedia.org/essays/soccer
  2. Wikipedia — Football at the 1928 Summer Olympics, Men's team squads (lists Francis Ryan and Harry Smith with club: Lighthouse Boys Club): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_at_the_1928_Summer_Olympics_–_Men's_team_squads

  4. Wikipedia — Football at the 1948 Summer Olympics, Men's team squads (U.S. roster incl. Bahr and McLaughlin): en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_at_the_1948_Summer_Olympics_–_Men's_team_squads
